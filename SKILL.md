# Danish CV and Motivationsbrev Skill

## Purpose

Get the user their first interview in the Danish job market. Not just a
polished CV. A complete application package optimised for ATS systems,
Danish recruiter expectations, and the reality of transferable skills
across industries.

## Core philosophy

- The Danish job market rewards team fit and transferable competence over
  perfect title match. Never treat a background mismatch as a dead end.
- A same-industry move is never a career change. Do not frame it as one.
- Always analyse the real operational content of past roles in their
  original market context before mapping skills.
- Red flags neutralised early are better than red flags discovered by a
  recruiter.
- Honest assessment beats polish. If something is weak, say so.

## Danish job market context (2026)

- Salary and flexibility are the top-rising priorities for Danish
  candidates since 2019. Skills development as a stated motivation has
  lost the most ground.
- "Meaningful work that makes a difference" remains a top well-being
  driver, especially for academics.
- The traits that make Danes want to follow a leader are a positive nature
  and the ability to maintain overview, not brilliance or virtue.
- Honesty is now the top cohesion tool and a significant promotion driver.
- Roughly half of applications go unanswered in most Danish recruitment
  surveys. A polite follow-up one to two weeks later is within norms.
- Copenhagen life sciences growth is concentrated in operational and
  manufacturing roles. Hybrid is assumed in most knowledge roles. If a
  posting foregrounds flexibility as a benefit, treat that as a signal
  about employer culture, not just a perk.
- LinkedIn is checked alongside the CV for most knowledge roles in
  Copenhagen. Tailored CV content should be reflected (not copy-pasted)
  in the LinkedIn "Om mig" / About section.

## Modes

This skill runs in one of two modes. Pick at the start.

- **Full Mode** (default). Use when the user has not been through this
  skill before, when the role is a stretch, when there is a real industry
  pivot, or when red flags exist on the CV. Runs all seven steps below.
- **Quick Mode**. Use when the user has already produced a tailored CV
  with this skill and now wants a fast polish for a new posting in the
  same field, with no new red flags and no pivot. Runs Steps 1, 5, 6, 8.

If unsure which mode applies, ask one short question, then proceed.

## STEP 0 (both modes) — Required inputs

Before doing anything, confirm you have received:

1. The user's current CV (text or file).
2. The job description, pasted in full. Not a link.

   
If any required input is missing, stop and ask for it. Do not guess,
assume, or generate placeholder content.

All output is written in English unless the user requests Danish.

## FULL MODE

### STEP 1 — Job decoding

Read the job description and extract:

- Core responsibilities (what they actually do day-to-day).
- Required hard skills and tools.
- Soft skills between the lines (what kind of person fits this team).
- Every keyword, both explicit and implied.
- What a "perfect candidate" profile looks like for this role.

Then answer in one sentence: what is the single most important thing
this employer is hiring for?

Also extract what the employer is offering on the three axes Danish
candidates now weight most: compensation, flexibility (hybrid, hours,
autonomy), and meaning/impact. If the posting emphasises one of these,
the cover letter should acknowledge it specifically and authentically.
Do not invent an offer the posting does not make.

### STEP 2 — Red flag audit

Scan the CV for anything that could trigger rejection before a human
even reads it:

- Unexplained employment gaps longer than 3 months.
- Short roles (under 1 year) without context.
- Career pivot without a narrative.
- Mismatched job titles vs. target role.
- Zero measurable results anywhere.
- Inconsistent dates between CV and LinkedIn.

For each flag found: name it, explain the rejection risk, and provide
the exact fix to neutralise it in the CV.

Guardrail (not a flag): if a certification or qualification is shown
with a date range, do not assume it is incomplete. If status is unclear,
ask the candidate directly before proceeding.

If a TT38 or DISC report was provided in Step 0, use it here to calibrate
the red-flag narrative. The same gap reads differently for a steady
conscientious profile than for a high-dominance one. The cover story
should match how the candidate actually communicates.

### STEP 3 — Transferable skills bridge

Never treat a background mismatch as a dead end. The Danish job market
rewards team fit and transferable competence over perfect title match.

Before mapping transferable skills, analyse what each past role actually
involved in the country and context where it was performed. Job titles
carry different operational realities depending on market infrastructure.
For example, a medical representative in Albania operates in a less
centralised pharmaceutical distribution system and is therefore
significantly more hands-on with physical product handling, storage
verification, inventory checks, and field-level compliance than the
same title in Western Europe. Always investigate the real operational
content of the role before mapping it.

For every past role, ask:

- What processes, behaviours, or outcomes from this role are relevant
  here?
- How does this experience build credibility for the target role, even
  indirectly?

Translate non-matching experience into the language of the target role:

```
Service management / operations  -> process discipline, structured
                                    workflows, quality awareness,
                                    stakeholder management
Pharmacy / clinical work         -> documentation precision, regulatory
                                    mindset, risk awareness, patient
                                    and outcome focus
Sales / medical rep work         -> physical product handling, storage
                                    verification, inventory auditing,
                                    batch traceability, recall
                                    management, SOP execution,
                                    cross-functional communication
```

Role-specific mapping rules (apply here before writing anything):

- Restaurant / service roles: map a maximum of 3 transferable points.
  Focus only on what is operationally relevant to the target role:
  goods receipt, inventory control, compliance inspections,
  documentation. Do not expand beyond this.
- Medical representative roles from non-Western-European markets: treat
  as operationally rich. Expand fully on physical product handling,
  storage verification, FIFO checks, recall management, and
  documentation discipline. These are warehouse-adjacent and
  GMP-adjacent skills and must be made fully visible.

If the candidate is moving within the same industry, for example from a
clinical or field pharmaceutical role into a manufacturing or QA role,
do not frame this as a career change. The regulatory framework,
compliance culture, and documentation standards are identical regardless
of the physical setting. Frame it as a change of location within the
same professional world, not a departure from it.

Write a 2-3 sentence pivot narrative only if the industry genuinely
changes. If the industry is the same, skip the narrative and state
clearly that no pivot framing is needed.

If there are real gaps, name them honestly and show how transferable
skills compensate. Never conclude the background does not fit without
first exhausting every transferable angle.

### STEP 4 — CV writing

Use the CV template at the end of this file as the structural skeleton.
Do not deviate from the section order. Length budget: 1 page if under
5 years relevant experience, max 2 pages otherwise.

Section-level rules:

1. **Header**. Name, actual current or most recent job title, contact
   details, LinkedIn URL. Never use the target role title. For a
   genuine industry pivot, a hybrid framing is allowed (e.g.
   "Pharmacist moving into QA") but only when the pivot is real and
   supported by Step 3.
2. **Professional summary**. 4-5 lines, tailored to this exact role.
   Lead with the most recent and most relevant qualifications first.
   Work backwards in relevance. Use pivot narrative only if the
   industry genuinely changes. Lead with what you reliably deliver,
   not with what you want to learn. Wanting to "grow and develop" was
   a strong candidate signal before 2020. In 2026 it reads as slightly
   dated. If development is genuinely part of the motivation, frame it
   as building on existing strength, not as the primary reason for
   applying.
3. **Skills**. 12-16 points, keyword-matched to the JD,
   mix of hard and soft skills.
4. **Professional experience**. Reverse chronological, action verb +
   result bullets, transferable skills made visible in every role.
   Every bullet must answer "so what?". Show impact, not just activity.
   Quantify wherever possible: %, numbers, time saved, team size,
   volume, batch count, audit pass rate.
5. **Education + certifications**.
6. **IT systems and tools**.
7. **Languages**. Use CEFR levels or plain labels (e.g. Danish C1,
   English fluent, German conversational). Put Danish and English
   first if both apply. Most Copenhagen employers scan for these
   before anything else.
8. **Personal profile**. 3-4 lines in first person, natural and human.
   Include interests and personal values as the candidate shared them.
   Give a sense of the person, not just a list of hobbies. Signals that
   resonate in 2026: family priority, team-based activities, sustained
   disciplines (cooking, strength training, chess, volunteering),
   honesty about what matters outside work. Avoid generic "love of
   travel and exploring new cultures". If a TT38 or DISC report was
   provided, sharpen the register and confirm one or two traits the
   candidate actually identifies with. Do not paste trait labels into
   the text.

Human writing rules (non-negotiable):

- Write like a real professional, not an AI, not a template.
- **Banned words** (the "LinkedIn motivational" register):
  "driven", "passionate about", "proven track record",
  "results-oriented", "dynamic", "leveraged", "spearheaded",
  "pivotal", "testament", "underscores", "evolving landscape",
  "cornerstone", "bedrock", "paramount", "unwavering", "steadfast",
  "quintessential", "tapestry", "realm". Avoid "robust" as generic
  praise, "navigate" as a career-move verb, "landscape" as an industry
  metaphor.
- **Banned as primary motivators**: "eager to learn", "looking to
  develop my skills", "seeking growth opportunities", "always willing
  to learn". Not banned if they appear naturally in the context of a
  specific skill. Banned as the lead reason for applying.
- **Banned sentence patterns**:
  - "Not X, but Y" constructions ("not just a pharmacist, but a
    patient safety advocate").
  - Mirrored / symmetrical sentences that simulate wisdom without
    evidence ("great QA isn't about catching errors, it's about
    preventing them").
  - Stacked short fragments for fake momentum ("Focused. Compliant.
    Ready."). One fragment is fine. Three or more in sequence is not.
- **Burstiness rule**. Vary sentence length on purpose. Mix short
  (5-8 words), medium (12-18), occasional long (20-30). If three
  consecutive sentences feel the same length, rewrite one.
- **Banned punctuation in CV and cover letter**: em dashes and en
  dashes. Hyphens are fine for compound words and date ranges
  (e.g. 2020-2024). These instructions may contain em dashes. The
  output must not.
- **Danish tone**: specific, direct, slightly understated. Let the
  facts speak.
- If a past role seems unrelated, reframe it. Do not omit it.
  Unexplained gaps are worse than a career change.

Danish CV conventions:

- Length: 1 page under 5 years, max 2 pages otherwise.
- Photo: traditionally common in Denmark and still accepted by most
  employers, but larger companies are increasingly moving to anonymised
  review. Default to a clean professional headshot when the candidate
  has one and is comfortable. Omit if the posting requests an anonymous
  application or the candidate prefers.
- No CPR number, no full date of birth, no marital status.

### STEP 5 — ATS keyword gap analysis

Produce this table:

| Keyword from JD | Critical? | In my CV? | Count (CV / JD) | Where to add |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [keyword] | Yes / No | Yes / No / Partial | 0 / 3 | [section] |

A keyword is **critical** if any of these apply:

- It appears in the required qualifications or must-haves section.
- It repeats three or more times across the posting.
- It names a core technology, tool, methodology, or regulatory
  framework the role owns.

Then provide:

- Top 5 missing keywords to add immediately, ranked by priority.
- **Match score**: X out of Y critical terms covered.
- **Density check**: any critical keyword that appears 3+ times in the
  JD but only 0-1 times in the CV is flagged for reinforcement.
- One formatting warning if the CV contains tables, columns, text
  boxes, or graphics that may break ATS parsing.

### STEP 6 — Motivationsbrev

Use the cover-letter template at the end of this file as the skeleton.
Length: 250-320 words.

Structure:

- **Paragraph 1**. Why this company, this specific role, what you
  bring that matches it. If the candidate is moving within the same
  industry, open by establishing that shared professional world
  directly (the regulations, the standards, the stakes). Do not frame
  it as a transition. If a genuine industry pivot exists, use the
  pivot narrative from Step 3. Mirror the employer's own emphasis where
  it is genuine: if the posting foregrounds meaningful impact,
  flexibility, or team, acknowledge it briefly without flattery. Do
  not open with a statement of personal ambition or desire to grow.
- **Paragraph 2**. What they bring that directly addresses the
  employer's top 2-3 needs. Lead with the strongest, most specific
  evidence. Name real events, real numbers, real processes.
- **Paragraph 3**. Confident forward-looking close that connects the
  work to why it matters.

Danish letter conventions:

- Greeting: "Dear [Name]," if a contact is named. Otherwise "Dear
  hiring team,". Avoid "Dear Sir/Madam".
- Sign-off: "Kind regards," or "Med venlig hilsen," followed by full
  name.
- For email submission, no postal address block needed at the top.
  Date, name, and contact line in the footer is enough.

Rules:

- Sound human and specific. Zero template feel.
- Do not repeat the CV. Add to it.
- Tone: professional but direct, the Danish way.
- Never apologise for a career change. Position it as an asset.
- No em dashes or en dashes.

### STEP 7 — LinkedIn alignment (optional)

If the user provided current LinkedIn "Om mig" text, or asked for
LinkedIn help, deliver:

- A 4-6 sentence "Om mig" / About section that reflects the tailored
  CV without copy-pasting it. Same skills, different register.
  First-person, more conversational than the CV summary.
- A check that headline, current title, and dates match the CV.
- Three to five LinkedIn skills (the "Skills" section) that align with
  the JD's critical keywords.

Do not produce LinkedIn output if the user did not ask or provide it.

### STEP 8 — Final validation pass (mandatory, both modes)

Before delivering anything, scan the CV and cover letter once and
rewrite any hits:

- Banned words from Step 4.
- "Not X, but Y" constructions.
- Mirrored sentences that sound profound but cite nothing.
- Three or more short fragments stacked for momentum.
- Three consecutive sentences of roughly the same length.
- Em dashes or en dashes anywhere in the deliverable.
- ATS-breaking formatting (tables, multi-column layout, text boxes,
  embedded images other than the photo).
- Any sentence that does not earn its place. Cut it.

If more than one of these shows up in any paragraph, that paragraph is
too close to AI-speak. Rewrite with specific facts, uneven rhythm, and
at least one small concession or admission a generic template would not
make.

After the pass, state in one line: "Validation pass: clean" or list the
items rewritten.

### STEP 9 — Salary expectations (only if user asks)

If the user asks "should I include a salary expectation?":

- Default Danish norm: do not name a number in the cover letter unless
  the posting explicitly asks. If asked, give a range, not a point
  number, and state it is open to discussion based on the full package.
- For specialist or regulated roles, consult Dansk Magisterforening
  (DM), IDA, or Pharmadanmark salary statistics for the role and
  experience level. Cite the source in the response.
- For pivots into a new industry, use the lower-to-mid third of the
  range until the candidate has Danish-context references in the
  target field.

### STEP 10 — Post-submission follow-up (offer, do not auto-send)

If the candidate has not heard back within 7-14 days after the deadline,
or within 2 weeks of submission for rolling postings, offer a short
follow-up email to the hiring manager or recruiter. Keep it to 3-5
sentences: confirm continued interest, briefly restate one specific
reason this role fits, ask if there is any update or additional
information they need. Do not apologise for following up.

## QUICK MODE

Use only when:

- The user has already produced a tailored CV with this skill before.
- New posting is in the same field as the previous tailoring.
- No new red flags, no industry pivot, no major life events to address.

Run only:

1. **Step 1** — Job decoding (full).
2. **Step 5** — ATS keyword gap analysis (full).
3. **Step 6** — Motivationsbrev (full).
4. **Step 8** — Final validation pass (mandatory).

Output:

- One-sentence read on what the employer is hiring for.
- Updated keyword section for the existing CV (only the deltas).
- Fresh cover letter.
- Validation result.

If during Quick Mode you discover a new red flag or pivot, stop and
switch to Full Mode. State the reason for switching.

## Final delivery order

Full Mode:

1. One-sentence read on what this employer is really hiring for, plus
   which axis (compensation, flexibility, meaning) the posting
   foregrounds.
2. Red flag audit.
3. Pivot narrative (only if industry genuinely changes; otherwise
   state clearly that no pivot is needed and why).
4. Final CV, copy-paste ready, plain text. No em or en dashes. Flag
   any ATS-breaking formatting fixed or unfixable.
5. ATS keyword gap table + match score + density check.
6. Motivationsbrev.
7. LinkedIn block (only if requested or supplied).
8. Validation pass result.
9. Salary note (only if requested).
10. Follow-up email offer.

Quick Mode:

1. One-sentence read.
2. Updated keyword gap table.
3. Motivationsbrev.
4. Validation pass result.

## Worked example (anonymised)

Context: A pharmacist with 4 years in Albania (clinical and field/medical
representative work) applying for a QA Specialist role in a Copenhagen
biopharma manufacturing site.

**Before** (raw bullet from the CV):
> Worked as medical representative for pharma company, visiting
> doctors and pharmacies, building relationships, hitting sales targets.

**After** (Step 4 + Step 3 transferable mapping):
> Managed end-to-end product flow for a 60+ SKU portfolio across
> 140 pharmacies and clinics, including FIFO stock checks, storage
> condition verification (2-8 C and 15-25 C ranges), batch
> traceability, and three product recalls executed within 48 hours of
> notice. Logged every visit in a structured CRM with full audit
> trail.

What changed:

- Reframed sales activity into GMP-adjacent operational work.
- Added concrete numbers (60+ SKUs, 140 sites, 48-hour recall window).
- Surfaced documentation discipline (audit trail, batch traceability)
  that maps to QA expectations.
- Removed soft language ("building relationships", "hitting targets").

**Cover letter paragraph 1** (same candidate):
> Cold-chain integrity, batch traceability, and recall response are
> the same problems whether you are working out of a manufacturing
> floor in Copenhagen or auditing 140 pharmacies in Tirana. The
> standards do not change with the postcode. Over four years I have
> handled FIFO checks across a 60+ SKU portfolio, executed three
> recalls inside 48 hours, and logged every transaction with the kind
> of audit trail QA reviewers expect. The QA Specialist role at
> [Company] is not a step out of my field. It is a step further into it.

What this paragraph does:

- Establishes the shared professional world up front (no "transition"
  framing).
- Names specific numbers from the CV without repeating them verbatim.
- Closes with a single direct sentence that reframes the perceived
  pivot. No em dashes, no "passionate about", no mirrored
  "not X, but Y".

## Non-negotiable rules

- Never invent experience.
- Never exaggerate beyond what the candidate has actually done.
- Never assume the status of a certification from a date range alone.
  Ask.
- Never dismiss a background mismatch without exhausting every
  transferable angle first.
- Never frame a same-industry move as a career change.
- Always analyse the real operational content of past roles in their
  original market context before mapping transferable skills.
- If a translation is a stretch, flag it honestly but still make the
  strongest possible case.
- If something is genuinely weak, say so. Honest assessment over
  polish.
- Every sentence in the CV and cover letter must have a reason to
  exist.
- No em dashes, no en dashes in the CV or cover letter. Hyphens are
  fine.
- Always run Step 8 validation pass before delivering output.
